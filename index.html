About me

Emma Piersanti is a professional writer interested in copy and substantive editing, publishing, and aspects of digital authoring. She is completing an English and Professional Writing degree at York University and writes fiction, academic pieces, as well as creative non-fiction. Featured works include her original piece “Time Well Spent”, which she published with York’s Annual Symposium in 2022. Through the symposium she worked together with other writers to create a cohesive collection of personal non-fiction stories and designed them to be viewed in a virtual gallery.

She is also a junior editor with the literary journal Existere, where she works with non-fiction writing as well as poetry. Emma is committed to finding significant stories from authors in the GTA and shining a light on them for others to discover. Emma often focuses on the tension-filled, yet beautiful changes experienced by young adults in her writing and how they can shape a person throughout those transitional phases. She values story-telling in all its forms, and is always interested in the power of writing to bring people together, highlight important voices, and transport readers to different, enticing realities.

                                          
                                                

Works

                                                

Time Well Spent

In this creative non-fiction piece, Emma explores the complications of transitioning from childhood to adulthood, and how this disorienting time can make you feel like you're the only one experiencing it. And as a result, how easy it is to become trapped in your own mind.

The Labyrinth of Language

There are secret worlds all around us. Worlds of words unknown to our ears. Of art that can whisper a message to only the keenest viewer. You may wonder, how can we access these worlds? The answer is language. In this piece, Emma delves into how languages have the power to lead us to new worlds through her experience discovering secret words, stories, and histories during her time in Italy.

Hunting Horcruxes

You may remember horcruxes from reading Harry Potter as a child. But this concept of immortality can be found in our every day, digital society. In the all-consuming, ever-present phenomenon of cancel culture. This essay examines the complex nature of cancel culture; specifically in regards to the creator of horcruxes herself: JK Rowling.
